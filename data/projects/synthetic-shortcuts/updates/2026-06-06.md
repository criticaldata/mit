**Meeting Purpose**

To "bush map" new research directions on synthetic data and model shortcuts.

**Key Takeaways**

- Synthetic data amplifies shortcuts: The core hypothesis is that synthetic data doesn't just replicate biases; it amplifies the spurious associations ("shortcuts") from the real data, leading to a false sense of model performance.
- Missingness is a care phenotype: Missing data is not random noise but a signal of care disparities. For example, patients with "unknown" race/ethnicity have higher mortality, explained by less data collection—a care phenotype that AI models can learn and exploit.
- New metrics are needed: Standard metrics like AUC are insufficient because they can be inflated by shortcut learning. The goal is to develop a "shortcut optimism" metric to measure how much of a model's performance is due to exploiting these spurious patterns.
- Research focus shifts to synthetic data: The team will prioritize proving the synthetic data hypothesis, deferring the core-set selection project. This work will span tabular (K-Mimic), image (MIMIC-CXR), and text data.

**Topics**

*The Problem: Synthetic Data Amplifies Shortcuts*

- Synthetic data is a flawed solution because it learns and amplifies the spurious associations ("shortcuts") present in the original data.
- Core Hypothesis: Models trained on synthetic data achieve high performance by exploiting these amplified shortcuts, not by learning the true underlying phenomena.
- The Risk: This creates a dangerous "false sense of accuracy," where a model appears to work well but relies on the wrong information.
- Initial Experiment Idea (Aldo):
  - Create a synthetic dataset from scratch with a known "real" pattern and a known "shortcut" pattern.
  - Train a model on this dataset and use explainability methods to verify it exploits the shortcut.
  - Generate synthetic data from this dataset, amplifying the shortcut.
  - Train a new model on the amplified data and confirm it relies even more heavily on the shortcut.

*The Root Cause: Missingness as a Care Phenotype*

- The team's research on missing data provides a key insight into the origin of these shortcuts.
- Finding: Patients with "unknown" race/ethnicity have higher mortality risk, even after adjusting for illness severity.
- Explanation: This is not random. It's a "care phenotype"—a signal of care disparities.
  - Patients perceived as "unknown" receive less data collection (e.g., fewer vital signs, lab tests).
  - This pattern of missingness is a direct result of how patients are treated.
- Implication: AI models can learn and exploit these care phenotypes as shortcuts, making predictions based on care disparities rather than clinical reality.
- Proposed Solution: Treat missingness patterns as a meaningful variable.
  - Cluster patients by their missingness patterns to create "missingness phenotypes."
  - Include these phenotypes in causal models (DAGs) to uncover hidden links between care processes and outcomes.

*The Solution: New Metrics & Research Focus*

- The team will develop new metrics to quantify shortcut learning, building on work by Gary Collins on "optimism" in model performance.
- Goal: Create a "shortcut optimism" metric to measure how much of a model's reported performance is due to exploiting spurious features.
- Research Focus: The team will prioritize proving the synthetic data hypothesis, deferring the core-set selection project.
- Scope: The experiments will span multiple data modalities:
  - Tabular: K-Mimic vs. K-Mimic Synthetic (Korean MIMIC).
  - Imaging: MIMIC-CXR.
  - Text: Synthetic doctor's notes from the French Health Data Hub.
