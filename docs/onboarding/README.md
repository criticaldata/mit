## Mission & culture

MIT Critical Data evolved from the [Laboratory for Computational Physiology (LCP)](intro-to-mark-lab-250317.md) at MIT. We democratize healthcare through open data, open software, and community-driven AI — uniting clinicians, data scientists, engineers, and researchers worldwide.

| Pillar | What we do |
|--------|------------|
| **Open data & software** | MIMIC, PhysioNet, and related tools |
| **Community capacity building** | Datathons, workshops, and collaborative research globally |
| **Equity advocacy** | Diversity and inclusion in AI research and development |

## Apply & join

### Steps

1. **Apply** — Complete the MIT Critical Data application process via [criticaldata.mit.edu](https://criticaldata.mit.edu/) (or directions you received from the lab).
2. **Attend onboarding** — Join an onboarding or intro session when invited. Session dates are not listed here; check lab communications or the [events calendar](../../data/events/README.md) for workshops and community gatherings.
3. **Come prepared** to share briefly:
   - **(a)** Your background (high school, undergrad, medical, graduate, clinical, technical, or community)
   - **(b)** Research interests and prior projects
   - **(c)** Skills you bring (clinical, data science, ML, writing, design, communications, community)
   - **(d)** Which [active project](../../data/projects/README.md) interests you most and why — browse the live directory rather than a static list

### What we look for in teams

Project teams should be **diverse** and ideally **5+ members**, mixing high school students, undergraduates, medical and graduate students, clinicians, engineers, data scientists, and community members.

## Choose a pathway

| Pathway | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Join a current project** | Contribute to an existing team matched to your skills and interests. Browse [**active projects →**](../../data/projects/README.md) |
| **Propose a new project** | Pitch something aligned with our mission. Approval depends on team fit, feasibility, and confirmation from lab leadership |

If you propose something new, describe the problem, who benefits, what evidence you need, and the team you can assemble. Final approval comes from the MIT Critical Data team after review.

## After you are onboarded

| # | Action |
|---|--------|
| 1 | Confirm your project assignment with the project lead and join the team's communication channel |
| 2 | Subscribe to the MIT Critical Data calendar so you do not miss sessions, journal clubs, or workshops |
| 3 | Track contributions: write-ups, audits, posts, dashboards, code, demos, screen recordings |
| 4 | Find a mentor or buddy on your project team; schedule recurring check-ins |
| 5 | Submit **weekly updates** so progress is visible and the team can support you |
| 6 | Read prior publications and project reports linked from the [highlights page](https://criticaldata.mit.edu/highlights) |
| 7 | Keep asking: **Who benefits from this research, and is the evidence representative of them?** |

### Contributing to this repository

Lab operations live in YAML under [`data/`](../../data/). When something changes — project status, team membership, event dates — update the relevant record and open a pull request. Meeting notes go in each record's `updates/` folder as `YYYY-MM-DD-description.md`.

See the [repository README](../../README.md) and [schema guide](../../CLAUDE.md) for field definitions and conventions.

When you onboard, your `person.yaml` profile and project links should appear in the generated tables within a day of merge (GitHub Actions regenerates summaries automatically).
